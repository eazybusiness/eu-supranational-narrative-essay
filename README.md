## Europe as Narrative Project: Legitimacy, Myth, and the Institutional Construction of a Supranational Identity

### Introduction

The history of European integration is not only a history of treaties, tariffs, and institutional architecture. It is also — and perhaps primarily — a history of meaning-making. From the earliest years of the postwar Community project, the institutions that emerged in Brussels, Luxembourg, and Strasbourg did not merely regulate trade or coordinate agricultural policy: they actively constructed a story about what Europe was, what it had suffered, and what it might become. This essay examines how those institutions built a supranational narrative capable of generating popular legitimacy in the absence of a unified European *demos*, tracing the mechanisms of symbolic appropriation, the foundational myths they propagated, and the structural limits of that project.

---

### I. Appropriating Europe: The Institutional Takeover of a Concept

The foundational move of the integration project was discursive: the identification of the European Community with "Europe" itself. As Kiran Klaus Patel has argued, the success of the EEC cannot be explained by its institutional novelty or its legal competences alone. What distinguished it from competing projects — the Council of Europe, the OEEC, various pan-European movements — was the way in which its advocates wrapped it in "an aura of expectations" and made it synonymous with the continent's destiny (Patel, 2013). By the 1970s, this appropriation had become so thorough that, as one oft-cited formulation has it, "Europe as a cultural entity exists only when people refer to Europe in the specific form of the EEC."

This was not accidental. Walter Hallstein, the Commission's first President (1958–1967), understood from the outset that the new institutions required a narrative scaffolding if they were to survive political resistance at the national level. His speeches — preserved in the Historical Archives of the EU in Florence — consistently framed the Community not as a technocratic arrangement but as the legitimate continuation of a European civilizational project interrupted by two world wars. The EEC was presented as the institutional answer to a historical question: how does Europe prevent itself from destroying itself again?

Thomas Risse's framework captures the deeper logic of this move. The EU, Risse argues, does not merely regulate European life — it *constitutes* "Europe" as a political and social reality (Risse, 2010). In other words, the institutional project is not simply built on top of a pre-existing European identity; it actively produces that identity through the accumulation of symbols, ceremonial practices, and shared reference points. The Community became the administrator of Europe's meaning.

---

### II. The Peace Narrative and Its Sacred Dimension

The most powerful and durable myth constructed by the integration project is also its simplest: the equation of European unity with the prevention of war. Few formulations crystallise this more starkly than Chancellor Helmut Kohl's declaration in 1994 that to speak of Europe was, at bottom, to speak of war or peace in the twenty-first century — a framing that invested the Community project with an almost theological urgency. The integration process, in this reading, was not a policy choice among several but a moral imperative with civilisational stakes.

This peace narrative drew its power from its rootedness in living memory. The generation that built the EEC in the 1950s had direct personal experience of Nazi occupation, aerial bombardment, and mass displacement. For figures such as Jean Monnet, Robert Schuman, and Alcide De Gasperi, the removal of economic and political rivalry between France and Germany was not an abstraction but a survival necessity. The narrative they fashioned — Europe as the institutionalisation of reconciliation — had the rare quality of being simultaneously instrumentally useful and emotionally genuine.

---

### III. Constitutional Narrative and the Court of Justice

The construction of Europe's legitimating narrative was not confined to political speeches and Commission brochures. The Court of Justice of the European Communities played a decisive, if often underappreciated, role. The landmark judgments of the early 1960s — *Van Gend en Loos* (1963) and *Costa v. ENEL* (1964) — did more than establish doctrines of direct effect and supremacy. They narrated a new constitutional order into being.

In *Van Gend en Loos*, the Court declared that the EEC Treaty constituted "a new legal order of international law for the benefit of which the States have limited their sovereign rights." This was not a legal finding in the conventional sense; it was a foundational story. The Court positioned itself — and the Community it served — as the guardian of a qualitatively new type of polity, one that derived its authority not from the consent of member states but from a direct relationship with European citizens. The rhetorical construction was as significant as the legal outcome.

---

### IV. The Limits of Supranational Myth-Making

Yet the supranational narrative project has never overcome the structural disadvantage that Vincent Della Sala identifies at its core: unlike the foundational myths of nation-states, the EU's myths lack an emotionally resonant narrative arc. National myths move through legible sequences — struggle, sacrifice, liberation, consolidation — and they are anchored in shared landscapes, languages, and memories. The European narrative, by contrast, is prospective rather than retrospective; it promises a future it cannot fully deliver and mourns a catastrophe (the World Wars) that successive generations remember less vividly.

The Adonnino Committee of 1985, tasked with developing proposals for a "People's Europe," diagnosed this deficit precisely. Its reports called for the creation of European symbols — a flag, an anthem, a passport, a founding day — in conscious imitation of the nation-state's toolkit. The very necessity of such a committee reveals the problem: no spontaneous European demos had emerged around the institutions. Legitimacy had to be manufactured.

---

### Conclusion (Excerpt)

The supranational narrative constructed by the EU institutions represents one of the most ambitious legitimation projects in modern political history. By appropriating the concept of "Europe" itself, forging a peace narrative anchored in catastrophic memory, and mobilising the Court of Justice as a constitutional storyteller, the Community project achieved a level of stability and acceptance that its early critics had considered impossible. Yet the limits of this project — its top-down character, its deficit of emotional depth, its dependence on a diminishing living memory of war — have become increasingly visible in an era of Euroscepticism and democratic backsliding.
